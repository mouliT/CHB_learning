\section{The CHB Converter: Circuit, Cascading, and the Balancing Problem}
\label{sec:circuit}
% ============================================================

\textit{This section builds the CHB from a single cell upward, shows why cascading
gives a better waveform, and identifies the core balancing problem that the paper
solves.}

The paper studies a
\textbf{grid-connected cascaded H-bridge (CHB)} converter where each cell has an
\textbf{isolated DC-link capacitor} and its own DC source.
Because different sources can deliver different power, the cell voltages naturally
drift apart unless the controller actively redistributes how much \emph{AC-side power}
each cell processes.

% ── §1.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{From One Cell to Many: How Cascading Works}
\label{subsec:cascading}

An H-bridge cell has two ports.
On the \textbf{DC side}, an isolated capacitor at voltage $\Vdci{i}$ is fed
by a private source (a PV string, battery, or rectifier) delivering power
$P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$.
On the \textbf{AC side}, four transistors chop that DC voltage at carrier
frequency $f_c \gg f$.
Averaged over one switching period, the fundamental component of the AC
output is:
\[
    \bar{v}_i(t) = \Vdci{i}\,\Mi\,\sin(\omg t)
\]
where $\Mi \in [0,1]$ is the modulation index --- the single knob the
controller turns to set how much power this cell contributes.

In reality the instantaneous $v_i(t)$ is a high-frequency PWM waveform; in this
tutorial we use the fundamental component because it is what transfers power to
the grid at frequency $f$.

Cascading means connecting the AC output terminals of $n$ cells in
\emph{series}.
The same grid current $\ig$ is forced through every cell.
The total voltage seen by the grid is:
\[
    \vg(t) = v_1(t) + v_2(t) + \cdots + v_n(t)
\]

\figentry{fig:staircase_buildup}{CHB_staircase_buildup}%
    {How three individual H-bridge cell outputs combine to form a
     seven-level multilevel waveform ($n = 3$, $f_c = 12f$, $M = 0.95$).
     \textbf{Rows 1--3:} each cell's unipolar PWM output (solid colour)
     with its phase-shifted triangular carrier (grey) and the shared
     sinusoidal reference (dashed).
     \textbf{Row 4 (Sum):} the series sum $\vg = v_1 + v_2 + v_3$
     steps through seven discrete levels and closely tracks the ideal
     sinusoid $3\Vdc M\sin(\omg t)$ (red dashed).
     The orange shading is the residual instantaneous error; phase-shifted
     carriers ensure no two cells switch simultaneously.}{1.0}

\figentry{fig:staircase_summation}{CHB_staircase_summation}%
    {Effect of increasing the number of cells $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$ on output
     waveform quality ($f_c = 6f$, $M = 0.95$).
     Each row shows the total output $\vg$ on a normalised axis
     $(\vg / n\Vdc)$.
     With $n = 1$ the output has only 3 levels and large harmonic error
     (orange shading).
     Each additional cell doubles the number of levels
     (3 $\to$ 5 $\to$ 7 $\to$ 9), shrinking the error region visibly.
     This is why CHB converters dominate high-power applications:
     more cells means lower harmonic distortion and a smaller filter $L_f$
     with no increase in individual transistor voltage rating.}{1.0}

% ── §1.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Full Circuit Topology}
\label{subsec:topology}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig1}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig1_page01}%
    {Grid-connected CHB converter with unbalanced loading powers
     $\PHi{1} \neq \PHi{2} \neq \PHi{3}$ (paper Fig.\,1).
     Each H-bridge cell has its own isolated DC-link capacitor fed by an
     independent DC source.
     The AC output terminals of all $n$ cells are connected in series;
     the series string drives the grid through filter inductance $L_f$.}{0.75}

\begin{circuitbox}[Component Walk-through (paper Fig.\,1)]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{DC sources (right side of each cell).}
          Each cell $i$ has a private DC source delivering $P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$
          to its isolated capacitor at $\Vdci{i}$.
          The dashed isolation boundary is not decorative:
          there is \emph{no} electrical connection between DC sides of
          different cells.

    \item \textbf{H-bridge switches (centre of each cell).}
          Four transistors per cell chop the DC voltage at carrier
          frequency $f_c$.
          The average AC output is
          $\bar{v}_i = \Vdci{i}\,\Mi\,\sin(\omg t)$.

    \item \textbf{AC series string (left column).}
          The top terminal of cell~$i$ connects to the bottom terminal
          of cell~$(i{+}1)$.
          Total output: $\vg = v_1 + \cdots + v_n$.

    \item \textbf{Filter inductor $L_f$.}
          Between the AC string and the grid.
          Attenuates PWM harmonics; forces $\ig$ to be approximately
          sinusoidal.
          Crucially, the \emph{same} $\ig$ flows through every cell ---
          there is no alternative current path.

    \item \textbf{Grid.}
          Modelled as $v_{\mathrm{grid}}(t) = V_{\mathrm{grid}}\sin(\omg t + \phig)$.
          The grid fixes frequency $\omg$ and power-factor angle $\phig$;
          the CHB controls amplitude and phase of $\vg$ to regulate $\ig$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{circuitbox}

% ── §1.3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Loading Power and the Capacitor Equation}
\label{subsec:loading_power}

Because the same current $\ig$ passes through every cell, each cell
\emph{must} process its share of AC power.
Each cell also has its own DC source pushing power in.
The net power that charges or discharges the DC-link capacitor of cell $i$
is its \textbf{loading power}:

\begin{eqnbox}[Definition of Loading Power]
\[
    \PHi{i} \;=\; \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} v_i(t)\,\ig(t)\,\mathrm{d}t
\]
where $T = 1/f$ is one grid period.
This is the average AC power \emph{delivered by cell $i$ to the grid}
(positive = delivering, negative = absorbing).
\end{eqnbox}

The paper calls this quantity the \textbf{cell loading power} (written as $P_{iH}$).

The DC-link capacitor stores energy $E_i = \tfrac{1}{2}C\Vdci{i}^2$.
Two flows act on it simultaneously: $P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$ in from the DC
source, and $\PHi{i}$ out to the AC string.
Their net difference gives the governing equation:

\begin{eqnbox}[Capacitor Energy Balance]
\[
    C\,\Vdci{i}\,\frac{d\Vdci{i}}{dt}
    \;=\; P_{i,\mathrm{src}} - \PHi{i}
\]
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{physbox}[Three consequences of this equation]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Steady state} ($\dot{V}_{dc,iH} = 0$) requires
          $\PHi{i} = P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$ every grid cycle.
    \item \textbf{Overcharging:} $P_{i,\mathrm{src}} > \PHi{i}$
          $\Rightarrow$ $\Vdci{i}$ rises.
    \item \textbf{Discharging:} $P_{i,\mathrm{src}} < \PHi{i}$
          $\Rightarrow$ $\Vdci{i}$ falls.
\end{enumerate}
This is why the paper focuses entirely on controlling $\PHi{i}$:
it is the only quantity the modulator can set to keep $\Vdci{i}$
at its target.
\end{physbox}

% ── §1.4 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{The Balancing Problem}
\label{subsec:problem}

\begin{warningbox}[Why DC voltages drift --- and why it matters]
The grid current $\ig$ is \emph{shared} by all cells.
The DC source powers $P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$ are \emph{independent}:
they depend on sunlight, battery state of charge, or deliberate power
routing, and can differ arbitrarily between cells.

If cell~1 receives more source power than cell~2, the only way to
prevent $\Vdci{1}$ from rising and $\Vdci{2}$ from falling is to
\emph{make cell~1 deliver more loading power than cell~2}.
But both cells share the same $\ig$ --- the only freedom is the
per-cell modulation index $\Mi$.

Left uncontrolled, one cell charges to destruction while another
discharges to zero. Balancing is therefore a hard requirement, not
an optional add-on.
\end{warningbox}

This imbalance occurs in every practical CHB application:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Photovoltaic strings} --- different panels see
          different shading.
    \item \textbf{Battery packs} --- cells age at different rates and
          reach different states of charge.
    \item \textbf{Power routing systems} --- cells are deliberately
          assigned unequal power to extend lifetime.
\end{itemize}

\section{The Control Architecture: Three Loops}
\label{sec:control}
% ============================================================

\textit{This section shows the complete control picture in one place --- the three
nested loops that govern the CHB --- and then explains the conventional PI
balancing method and why it is slow.}

% ── §2.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{The Three-Layer Control Scheme}
\label{subsec:three_loops}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig2}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig2_page02}%
    {Three-layer control scheme for the grid-connected CHB converter
     (paper Fig.\,2).
     \textbf{Outer loop (DC-link voltage control, slow):} compares the
     average DC-link voltage to its reference and generates the grid
     current reference $\ig^{*}$.
     \textbf{Inner loop (grid current control, fast):} forces the actual
     grid current to track $\ig^{*}$ and produces the total voltage
     reference $\vref$.
     \textbf{Balancing layer (per-cell):} divides $\vref$ among the $n$
     cells by computing individual modulation indices $M_i$ (and clamping
     angles $\phic$ in the proposed method).}{0.9}

\begin{circuitbox}[Three-Layer Walk-through (paper Fig.\,2)]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Outer loop --- DC-link voltage control (slow).}
          Measures the average DC-link voltage
          $\bar{V}_{dc} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \Vdci{i}$,
          compares it to the reference $V_{dc}^{*}$, and passes the error
          through a PI controller.
          Output: grid current reference $\Ig^{*}$.
          This loop is slow (bandwidth $\approx f_{bal}$) because it
          must not disturb the inner current loop.

    \item \textbf{Inner loop --- grid current control (fast).}
          Measures the actual grid current $\ig$, compares it to $\Ig^{*}$,
          and passes the error through another PI (or resonant) controller.
          Output: total voltage reference $\vref$.
          This loop is fast (bandwidth $\gg f$) to achieve low current THD.

    \item \textbf{Balancing layer --- per-cell modulation.}
          Takes $\vref$ and the measured individual voltages $\Vdci{i}$,
          and computes a per-cell output reference $v_{iH}^{*}$ for each cell.
          In the conventional method this is done with per-cell PI
          controllers (paper Fig.\,3).
          In the proposed method it is done by the sorting algorithm
          with clamped/non-clamped modulation (Sections~\ref{sec:proposed}
          and~\ref{sec:algorithm}).
\end{enumerate}
\end{circuitbox}

The key constraint linking all three layers is the \emph{coupling constraint}:
all cells share the same $\ig$, so their voltage references must sum to $\vref$:

\begin{eqnbox}[Coupling Constraint]
\[
    \sum_{i=1}^{n} v_{iH}^{*} \;=\; \vref
    \qquad
    \Longleftrightarrow
    \qquad
    \sum_{i=1}^{n} \Vdci{i}\,\Mi \;=\; \vref
\]
Any increase in one cell's $\Mi$ must be compensated by decreases in
the others, or the total voltage reference is violated.
This coupling is what makes balancing non-trivial.
\end{eqnbox}

% ── §2.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Conventional PI Balancing --- The Method This Paper Replaces}
\label{subsec:conventional_pi}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig3}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig3_page02}%
    {Conventional PI-based DC-link voltage balancing (paper Fig.\,3).
     Each cell $i$ has a dedicated PI controller that compares
     $\Vdci{i}$ to the average voltage $\bar{V}_{dc}$ and adds a
     correction $\Delta v_{iH}$ to the shared voltage reference $\vref$,
     giving a per-cell reference $v_{iH}^{*} = \vref + \Delta v_{iH}$.
     All $n$ correction terms must sum to zero to satisfy the coupling
     constraint.}{0.9}

\begin{circuitbox}[How Conventional PI Balancing Works]
Each cell runs an independent PI loop:
\[
    v_{iH}^{*}(k) \;=\; \vref(k) \;+\; \Delta v_{iH}(k)
\]
where $\Delta v_{iH}$ is proportional-plus-integral of the individual
voltage error $(\Vdci{i} - \bar{V}_{dc})$.

The controller must respect the coupling constraint
$\sum_i \Delta v_{iH} = 0$: if one cell's correction increases,
another's must decrease by the same amount.
\end{circuitbox}

\textbf{Why it is slow.}
The PI bandwidth cannot be set freely.
There is a strict hierarchy of time constants:
\[
    t_{sw} \;\ll\; t_{c} \;\ll\; t_{v} \;\ll\; t_{bal}
\]
where $t_{sw}$ is the switching period, $t_c$ the current-loop bandwidth,
$t_v$ the voltage-loop bandwidth, and $t_{bal}$ the balancing settling time.
The balancing loop must be \emph{slower} than the voltage loop to avoid
destabilising it.
This intrinsic hierarchy means the PI controller can only correct a tiny
fraction of the voltage imbalance each grid cycle.
Result: settling time $\approx 1850$\,ms (92 grid cycles at 50\,Hz).

\begin{warningbox}[The fundamental limitation of PI balancing]
The PI adds a small $\Delta v_{iH}$ each cycle.
The maximum step size is limited by the requirement not to distort the
grid current.
No matter how aggressively the gains are tuned, the settling time is
bounded below by the bandwidth hierarchy.
This is the limitation the paper overcomes --- not by better PI tuning,
but by replacing the architecture entirely.
\end{warningbox}

\section{The Proposed Method: Modulation-Based Balancing}
\label{sec:proposed}
% ============================================================

\textit{This section rebuilds the proposed method in the same order as the IEEE
paper: first the principle (two complementary modulation signals), then how it is
updated in time, and finally why it increases the range of achievable loading
power.}

% ── §3.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Principle: Two Complementary Modulation Signals}
\label{subsec:principle}

The inner current controller (Section~\ref{sec:control}) produces a
\emph{sinusoidal} reference signal $v^{*}$.
In the paper, $v^{*}$ is a \textbf{normalised} signal (bounded within the linear
modulation region), and $n\,v^{*}$ represents the total normalised reference of
the full CHB string.
If the sum of the cell outputs follows this reference, the grid current can be
kept sinusoidal.
The key trick in the paper is to let \emph{individual} cells use a discontinuous
signal, as long as the \emph{sum across cells} is still sinusoidal.

The method defines two operation modes for a cell: \textbf{clamped} and
\textbf{non-clamped}.
The clamped modulation signal depends only on the sign of the grid voltage:

\begin{eqnbox}[Clamped and Non-Clamped Signals (paper Eq.\,2--3)]
\[
v_{c}^{*} 
=
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \vg > 0 \\
-1 & \text{if } \vg < 0
\end{cases}
\qquad
v_{nc}^{*} = n\,v^{*} - v_{c}^{*}
\]
\[
v_{c}^{*} + v_{nc}^{*} = n\,v^{*}
\]
\end{eqnbox}

Read this as a construction rule:
the algorithm creates two waveforms whose combined effect preserves the sinusoidal
total reference demanded by the inner current loop.

\figentry{fig:paper_fig4}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig4_page03}%
    {Principle of the proposed method (paper Fig.\,4).
     Measured cell voltages are sorted and compared to the reference.
     Based on the sign of $\vg\ig$, cells are assigned either clamped
     ($v_{c}^{*}$) or non-clamped ($v_{nc}^{*}$) modulation.
     The key constraint is that the sum of all cell modulation signals remains
     sinusoidal (paper Eq.\,3).}{0.9}

\begin{physbox}[Why this helps balancing]
In conventional PI balancing, each cell stays sinusoidal and can only change its
loading power by changing its sinusoidal amplitude (bounded by $|M|\le 1$).
The proposed method adds a new lever: \emph{waveform shape}.
By clamping some cells to $\pm 1$ for part of the cycle, the method changes the
fundamental component of the cell voltage, and therefore changes the average
loading power it can process.
\end{physbox}

% ── §3.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{When the Algorithm Updates (and Why It Can Be Fast)}
\label{subsec:implementation_overview}

The balancing decision is recomputed periodically with update period $t_{bal}$.
The paper chooses $t_{bal}$ such that it is a divisor of the grid period:
\[
t_f = a\,t_{bal}, \quad a \in \mathbb{Z}
\]
so the assignment can change multiple times within one grid cycle.

\begin{statebox}[The speed advantage in one sentence]
In the conventional PI method, the balancing dynamics are constrained by the
outer voltage loop bandwidth (the hierarchy $t_{sw} \ll t_c \ll t_v \ll t_{bal}$).
In the proposed method, the balancing update $t_{bal}$ is not tied to $t_v$ and
$t_c$ in the same way, so it can be made much faster.
\end{statebox}

This section only establishes \emph{what} is updated and \emph{when}.
The exact sorting and assignment rules (including the role of the sign of
$\vg\ig$ and the multi-cell pairing cases) are the topic of
Section~\ref{sec:algorithm}.

% ── §3.3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Why Clamping Increases the Loading-Power Range}
\label{subsec:capability}

The average loading power of a cell depends on how its voltage waveform lines up
with the (approximately sinusoidal) grid current over one grid period.
The important point is that only the \textbf{fundamental component} of the cell
voltage contributes to the cycle-average power exchange; higher harmonics average
to zero over a full period.

To quantify this, the paper introduces a \textbf{clamping angle} $\phic$ and varies
it from $0$ to $\pi$ (0 to 180 degrees) (paper Fig.\,7).
In this tutorial we will place and walk through paper Fig.\,7 and paper Fig.\,8 in
Section~\ref{sec:algorithm} so the figures appear in the same sequence as the
paper's narrative.

The paper computes the fundamental magnitude using Fourier series.
For a periodic signal $f(\theta)$, the fundamental coefficients are:

\begin{eqnbox}[Fundamental From Fourier Coefficients (paper Eq.\,4--5)]
\[
a_1 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} f(\theta)\cos\theta\,d\theta,
\qquad
b_1 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{0}^{2\pi} f(\theta)\sin\theta\,d\theta
\]
\[
F = \sqrt{a_1^2 + b_1^2}
\]
\end{eqnbox}

Using the piecewise clamped waveform (paper Eq.\,6) with nominal modulation index
$M$ (paper notation):

\begin{eqnbox}[Piecewise Clamped Waveform (paper Eq.\,6)]
\[
f_c(\theta)=
\begin{cases}
1 & \theta\in[0,\phic]\\
M\cos\theta & \theta\in[\phic,\pi] \text{ and } \theta\in[\pi+\phic,2\pi]\\
-1 & \theta\in[\pi,\pi+\phic]
\end{cases}
\]
\end{eqnbox}

the paper derives the fundamental magnitude of the clamped signal $F_c(\phic)$ and
the complementary non-clamped signal $F_{nc}(\phic)$ (paper Eq.\,7):

\begin{eqnbox}[Fundamental Magnitudes (paper Eq.\,7)]
\[
F_c(\phic) = \frac{1}{\pi}
\sqrt{\left(0.5M\sin(2\phic) - M\phic - 2\cos\phic + \pi M + 2\right)^2
+ \left(\sin\phic\,(2 - M\sin\phic)\right)^2}
\]
\[
F_{nc}(\phic) = 2M - F_c(\phic)
\]
\end{eqnbox}

\begin{statebox}[Numbers to remember from paper Fig.\,8]
At $M=0.7$ and $\phic=\pi$ (180 degrees), the paper reports approximately
1.82 p.u. loading power in clamped mode and 0.18 p.u. in non-clamped mode.
That large spread is the ``extra room'' the algorithm uses to move energy between
cells quickly.
\end{statebox}

\section{The Sorting Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}
% ============================================================

\textit{This section shows exactly how the algorithm runs each balancing period:
which cell gets clamped, how the sign of grid power determines the direction,
and how the assignment extends from two cells to $N$ cells.}

% ── §4.1 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Sorting Principle and Sign Rule}
\label{subsec:sorting}

At each balancing period, the algorithm:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Measures all DC-link voltages $\Vdci{i}$.
    \item Computes the sign of the instantaneous grid power $\vg\ig$
          to determine whether the CHB is in rectifier or inverter mode.
    \item Ranks cells by $\Vdci{i}$ and assigns modes based on rank and sign.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{statebox}[Sign Rule for Mode Assignment]
\textbf{Inverter mode} ($\vg\ig > 0$, CHB delivering power to grid):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cells with \emph{high} $\Vdci{i}$ (overcharged)
          $\to$ \textbf{clamped} (deliver more power, discharge faster).
    \item Cells with \emph{low} $\Vdci{i}$ (undercharged)
          $\to$ \textbf{non-clamped} (deliver less power, recover slower).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rectifier mode} ($\vg\ig < 0$, CHB absorbing power from grid):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Cells with \emph{low} $\Vdci{i}$ (undercharged)
          $\to$ \textbf{clamped} (absorb more power, charge faster).
    \item Cells with \emph{high} $\Vdci{i}$ (overcharged)
          $\to$ \textbf{non-clamped} (absorb less power, charge slower).
\end{itemize}

In both cases, the rule drives all voltages toward the average.
\end{statebox}

% ── §4.2 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Two-Cell Case Study}
\label{subsec:two_cell}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig5}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig5_page03}%
    {Two-cell case study showing DC-link voltage balancing with
     $t_{bal} = 5$\,ms (paper Fig.\,5).
     The simulation is divided into four regions (I--IV) by the sign of
     $\vg\ig$ and the relative ordering of $\Vdci{1}$ and $\Vdci{2}$.
     \textbf{Region I ($\vg\ig > 0$, $\Vdci{1} > \Vdci{2}$):}
         Cell~1 is clamped (overcharged, discharging fast), cell~2 is
         non-clamped (undercharged, recovering slowly).
     \textbf{Region II ($\vg\ig < 0$, $\Vdci{1} > \Vdci{2}$):}
         Sign reversal --- cell~2 is now clamped to charge faster,
         cell~1 non-clamped.
     \textbf{Regions III--IV:} voltages have crossed;
         assignment swaps to continue driving toward balance.
     The DC-link voltages converge to the common target within
     $\approx 140$\,ms.}{0.9}

Walking through each region of \figref{fig:paper_fig5}:

\begin{circuitbox}[Region-by-Region Analysis (Two-Cell Case)]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Region I} ($\vg\ig > 0$, inverter mode;
          $\Vdci{1} > \Vdci{2}$).
          Cell~1 is overcharged $\Rightarrow$ clamped:
          it delivers extra loading power and discharges toward the
          average.
          Cell~2 is undercharged $\Rightarrow$ non-clamped:
          it delivers less power and its voltage recovers.
          Both voltages move toward each other.

    \item \textbf{Region II} ($\vg\ig < 0$, rectifier mode;
          still $\Vdci{1} > \Vdci{2}$).
          The grid power sign has flipped.
          Now the rule for rectifier mode applies:
          undercharged cell (cell~2) is clamped to absorb more power
          and charge faster; overcharged cell (cell~1) is non-clamped.
          Balancing continues in the same direction.

    \item \textbf{Regions III--IV.}
          Eventually $\Vdci{1}$ and $\Vdci{2}$ cross (or converge close
          enough that the ranking reverses).
          The algorithm detects the new rank and swaps assignments.
          The back-and-forth is fast: the $\approx 140$\,ms settling
          covers roughly 7 grid cycles.
\end{enumerate}
\end{circuitbox}

% ── §4.3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Four-Cell Case: Five Operation Modes}
\label{subsec:four_cell}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig6}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig6_page04}%
    {Five operation modes for the four-cell ($n = 4$) case
     (paper Fig.\,6).
     Each mode is defined by which cell(s) are clamped and which are
     non-clamped.
     \textbf{Mode 1:} all cells non-clamped (balanced steady state).
     \textbf{Mode 2:} one cell clamped, three non-clamped.
     \textbf{Mode 3:} two cells clamped, two non-clamped.
     \textbf{Mode 4:} three cells clamped, one non-clamped.
     \textbf{Mode 5:} all cells clamped (maximum imbalance correction).
     The algorithm selects the mode with the minimum number of clamped
     cells that can still achieve balance, so as to minimise waveform
     distortion.}{0.9}

For $n > 2$ cells the algorithm must decide not only \emph{which}
cells are clamped but also \emph{how many}.
\figref{fig:paper_fig6} shows the five modes possible for $n = 4$:

\begin{statebox}[Mode Selection Rule]
Starting from Mode~1 (all non-clamped), increment the mode
(add one more clamped cell) until the target loading powers
$\PHi{i}^{*} = P_{i,\mathrm{src}}$ for all cells can be satisfied
simultaneously within the coupling constraint
$\sum_i \Vdci{i}\,\Mi = \vref$.

The minimum-mode strategy limits the distortion introduced by clamping
while still achieving full balance each period.
\end{statebox}

\begin{physbox}[General $N$-Cell Summary]
For $N$ cells with loading powers sorted as
$P_{1,\mathrm{src}} \geq P_{2,\mathrm{src}} \geq \cdots \geq P_{N,\mathrm{src}}$
(inverter mode, cells ranked by descending $\Vdci{i}$):
\begin{itemize}
    \item The $k$ most overcharged cells receive clamped modulation.
    \item The remaining $N-k$ cells receive non-clamped modulation.
    \item $k$ is the smallest integer such that the power targets can
          all be met.
    \item At each update, $k$ can be 0 (all sinusoidal) up to $N$ (all clamped).
\end{itemize}
\end{physbox}

\subsection{Experimental: Dynamic Transient --- The Payoff Figure}
\label{subsec:exp_transient}

\figentry{fig:paper_fig14}{DLVBMCHBC_Fig14_page08}%
    {Dynamic transient comparison: conventional PI (left) vs.\ proposed
     method (right) for a step imbalance in DC source power
     (paper Fig.\,14).

     (92 grid cycles).

\begin{eqnbox}[The Key Result]
\[
    \boxed{
    \text{Conventional PI: } 1850\,\text{ms}
    \quad\longrightarrow\quad
    \text{Proposed method: } 140\,\text{ms}
    \quad\bigl(13\times \text{ faster}\bigr)
    }
\]
Both methods reach the same steady-state outcome:
balanced DC-link voltages and sinusoidal grid current.
The only difference is how fast they get there --- and the difference
is large enough to be the primary engineering contribution of the paper.
\end{eqnbox}

The speed advantage comes entirely from the change in architecture:
instead of nudging $\Mi$ by $\Delta M_i$ each cycle (PI),
the algorithm computes the exact $\Mi^{*}$ and $\phic^{*}$ that
achieve balance and applies them in \emph{one} balancing period.
The loading power closed-form expressions derived in
Section~\ref{sec:proposed} make this one-step inversion possible.
